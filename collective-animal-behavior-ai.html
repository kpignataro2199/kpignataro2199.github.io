Beyond Individual Animals: How AI and Next-Generation Sensors Are Reshaping Collective Behaviour Research

March 1, 2026

In recent years, AI’s role in animal studies has expanded from identifying species and individuals to addressing more complex questions: how groups of animals behave as coordinated units. A growing body of research shows that machine learning and advanced sensing tools are now being applied not just to monitor individual animals, but to analyze collective behaviour — the ways in which animals move, communicate, and coordinate as a group. These advances promise to deepen our scientific understanding of social dynamics and ecosystem responses.

What Is Collective Behaviour — and Why AI?

Collective behaviour refers to how groups of animals interact and coordinate their actions at the group level. Examples include flocking in birds, schooling in fish, herding in ungulates, and swarming in insects. These patterns emerge from repeated interactions among individuals and are often sensitive to environmental conditions.

Studying collective behaviour has historically required painstaking manual observation and coding. Today, AI enables researchers to track every individual in a group over time while deriving metrics that capture group-level dynamics. This is vital because collective behaviours often contain signals of stress or environmental change that aren’t visible when focusing solely on individuals.

AI + Sensors: The Technological Backbone

High-resolution cameras, lidar, and next-generation environmental sensors can capture rich data across space and time. AI models then process this data to extract behavioral features such as movement trajectories and spatial organization. For example, vision transformers can reconstruct individual positions within a group even when animals occlude one another.

Why This Matters for Welfare and Ecology

  • Early detection of stress or disease: Changes in group cohesion can signal discomfort before individual animals show signs.
  • Habitat disturbance monitoring: Variations in movement patterns may reflect disruptions due to human activity.
  • Behavioral flexibility: AI allows quantification of how species shift collective strategies in response to predation or stress.

Challenges and Ethical Considerations

Processing collective behaviour data requires enormous computational capacity. Furthermore, AI systems trained on one species often don’t generalize well to others without retraining. Ethically, automated surveillance can inadvertently collect sensitive data on endangered or domesticated animals. Integrating animal interests into AI development is necessary for responsible innovation.

My Perspective: One of the most exciting aspects of this trend is how it bridges individual and group behaviour. AI’s ability to process and interpret group dynamics at scale means researchers can now observe the forest and the trees simultaneously — something that was previously impractical. This shift allows us to detect patterns that emerge only at the group level, potentially leading to more humane livestock management and better wildlife protection.
