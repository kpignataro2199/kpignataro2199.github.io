Beyond Individual Animals: How AI and Next-Generation Sensors Are Reshaping Collective Behaviour Research

March 1, 2026

In recent years, AI’s role in animal studies has expanded from identifying species and individuals to addressing more complex questions: how groups of animals behave as coordinated units[cite: 2]. A growing body of research shows that machine learning and advanced sensing tools are now being applied not just to monitor individual animals, but to analyze collective behaviour — the ways in which animals move, communicate, and coordinate as a group[cite: 3]. These advances promise to deepen our scientific understanding of social dynamics and ecosystem responses[cite: 4].

What Is Collective Behaviour — and Why AI?

Collective behaviour refers to how groups of animals interact and coordinate their actions at the group level, such as flocking in birds or herding in ungulates[cite: 6, 7]. These patterns emerge from repeated interactions among individuals and are often sensitive to environmental conditions[cite: 8]. Today, AI enables researchers to track every individual in a group over time while deriving metrics that capture group-level dynamics[cite: 10]. This is vital because collective behaviours often contain signals of stress or environmental change that aren't visible when focusing solely on individuals[cite: 11].

AI + Sensors: The Technological Backbone

High-resolution cameras, lidar, and next-generation environmental sensors can capture rich data across space and time[cite: 14]. For example, vision transformers and motion-aware tracking algorithms can reconstruct individual positions within a group and maintain consistent tracking even when animals occlude one another[cite: 17]. Multimodal architectures are also emerging that integrate visual, audio, and contextual data to better resolve behaviour in complex environments[cite: 18].

Why This Matters for Welfare and Ecology

Collective behaviour can offer insights that individual metrics miss[cite: 21]. Changes in group cohesion can signal discomfort or disease outbreaks before individual animals show obvious signs[cite: 23]. Additionally, variations in collective movement patterns may serve as early indicators of ecosystem change due to human activity or environmental stressors[cite: 24].

Challenges and Ethical Considerations

Processing collective behaviour data requires enormous computational capacity, and models often don't generalize well across different species without substantial retraining[cite: 28, 29]. Ethically, researchers must consider how these technologies are deployed, as automated surveillance can inadvertently collect sensitive data on endangered or domesticated populations[cite: 30, 31]. Recent arguments suggest animals are largely absent from ethical AI discourses, even though these technologies increasingly shape their welfare[cite: 33].

My Perspective: A New Frontier in Behaviour Science
One of the most exciting aspects of this trend is how it bridges individual and group behaviour[cite: 36]. Traditional ethology often isolates individuals, but many species’ most important behaviours occur in social contexts[cite: 37]. AI’s ability to process and interpret group dynamics at scale means researchers can now observe the forest and the trees simultaneously[cite: 38]. This shift allows us to detect patterns and early warning signs that emerge only at the group level, potentially leading to more humane management and richer ecological insights[cite: 39, 40].
